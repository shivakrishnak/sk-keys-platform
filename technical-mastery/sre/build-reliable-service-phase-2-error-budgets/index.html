⚡ TL;DR - Phase 2 of the Build a Reliable Service series moves from SLO measurement (Phase 1, SRE-043) to error budget policy enforcement. The deliverables: an error budget dashboard showing remaining budget and burn rate, an error budget policy document that defines what happens when budget reaches 10%, 5%, and 0%, and a sprint planning integration that makes error budget status visible in backlog prioritization. After Phase 2: the team has an SLO that drives organizational behavior, not just a number on a dashboard.

🔥 The Problem This Solves

THE “SLO DASHBOARD NOBODY USES” PROBLEM:

After Phase 1: the team has an SLO. The dashboard shows: “Payment success rate: 99.95% (target: 99.9% - PASSING).” Team members look at the dashboard once a week. It passes. Nobody changes their behavior. Feature work continues at full speed. One week: a database migration deploy causes a 45-minute degradation. The dashboard now shows: “Payment success rate: 99.87% (target: 99.9% - FAILING).” The SLO is violated. Nobody did anything differently when the error budget was being consumed. There was no signal to slow down feature releases, no policy that triggered when budget hit 20%, no sprint planning visibility that made reliability risk visible to the PM.

The SLO exists. The error budget mechanism does not.

Phase 2 transforms the SLO from a measurement into a management tool that drives organizational behavior.

📘 The Three Phase 2 Deliverables

PHASE 2 DELIVERABLES OVERVIEW

  DELIVERABLE 1: ERROR BUDGET DASHBOARD
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  What it shows:
  → Remaining error budget (% and minutes)
  → Current burn rate (fast: past 1h; slow: past 72h)
  → Budget consumption by incident type
  → Budget trend (at current burn rate: days until exhaustion)
  → Historical budget consumption by sprint
  
  DELIVERABLE 2: ERROR BUDGET POLICY DOCUMENT
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  What it defines:
  → At 20% budget remaining: reliability review triggered
  → At 10% budget remaining: high-risk deploys require approval
  → At 5% budget remaining: feature freeze begins
  → At 0% budget remaining: reliability sprint mandatory
  → Policy reset: new budget available at window reset (monthly)
  → Override conditions: when policy can be suspended and by whom
  
  DELIVERABLE 3: SPRINT PLANNING INTEGRATION
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  What it adds to sprint planning:
  → Opening status check: remaining error budget for each SLO
  → Reliability work allocation: if budget < 20%, reserve 30%
    of sprint for reliability work
  → Deploy risk assessment: tag high-risk deploys; team reviews
    whether budget can absorb them
  → End-of-sprint report: budget consumed this sprint vs. target

⏱️ Understand It in 30 Seconds

One line: Phase 2 builds the error budget management infrastructure: the dashboard that shows remaining budget, the policy document that defines what happens when budget is low, and the sprint planning integration that makes error budget visible when teams decide what to build next.

One analogy:

Phase 1 (SLO measurement) was installing a fuel gauge in a car. Phase 2 (error budget policy) is configuring the warning lights and the behavior when the tank is low. Without Phase 2: you have a gauge that shows how much fuel is left but no alarm when it drops to 15%, no dashboard warning at 5%, and no instruction for what to do when it reads empty. With Phase 2: at 15% fuel, a warning lights up. At 5%, a dashboard alert says “Find fuel soon.” At empty: the car does not start (feature freeze). The gauge (SLO) is the same. The behavior it drives is completely different.

📶 Gradual Depth - Five Levels

Level 1 - The simplest error budget dashboard: A minimal error budget dashboard needs two metrics: (1) budget remaining (% of error budget not yet consumed in the current window); (2) current burn rate (how fast is the budget being consumed relative to the SLO rate). With just these two numbers visible on a shared dashboard (a TV monitor in the team’s work area, or a persistent Slack message), the team has the visibility to make informed decisions. “Budget remaining: 45%, burn rate: 1.2x (normal)” means everything is fine. “Budget remaining: 8%, burn rate: 4.3x (high)” means something is actively consuming budget and reliability work is needed.

Level 2 - Writing the error budget policy that teams will follow: An error budget policy fails if it is too abstract (“when budget is low, prioritize reliability”) or too draconian (“budget at 5% = complete feature freeze, no exceptions”). The most effective policies have: (1) specific, measurable thresholds (not “low” but “< 20%”); (2) specific, actionable responses (not “prioritize reliability” but “20% of sprint reserved for reliability work”); (3) an override path (the policy can be suspended with named approvals, which creates accountability without rigidity); (4) a reset mechanism (monthly budget reset means the policy state is temporary).

Level 3 - Making the policy real in sprint planning: The critical moment is the first sprint planning meeting where an engineer says “our error budget is at 8% - we should not deploy the database migration this sprint.” This moment will be uncomfortable. A PM will push back. This is the test of whether the error budget policy has organizational support. Preparation: before implementing the policy, present it to the PM and engineering lead. Get explicit agreement on the feature freeze threshold and override process. “We agree that if budget < 10%, we will pause features. The override is a joint decision between VP Engineering and SRE Lead.” Getting agreement before the first friction point makes the policy stick.

Level 4 - The budget consumption report: Each sprint, produce a 1-page budget consumption report: how much budget was consumed this sprint, what consumed it (incident list with budget minutes), and whether the trend is on track for a healthy monthly reset. This report makes the cost of incidents visible in units that the business cares about: “INC-301 (database migration failure) consumed 42% of our monthly error budget. At this rate, we will exhaust the budget in 8 days.” This framing changes the business conversation: incidents are not just technical events; they consume a finite reliability resource that enables feature velocity.

Level 5 - Error budget as a resource allocation tool: The most sophisticated use of error budget is as a resource allocation signal: teams with high remaining error budget (>70%) are healthy and can invest in feature work. Teams with low remaining budget (<20%) need reliability investment. At scale (many teams, many services), the error budget remaining across all teams is a portfolio-level signal: “3 of our 12 services are in Warning state, 1 is in Feature Freeze. These 4 services need reliability investment this quarter.” This portfolio view enables organization-level reliability resource allocation - which is what Phase 3 (SRE-093) and Phase 4 (SRE-110) build upon.

from datetime import datetime, timedelta
from typing import Optional


@dataclass
class SLOConfig:
    service_name: str
    slo_target: float  # e.g., 0.999 for 99.9%
    window_days: int   # e.g., 28
    traffic_rps: Optional[float] = None  # Requests per second


@dataclass
class ErrorBudgetState:
    slo_config: SLOConfig
    window_start: datetime
    total_budget_minutes: float
    consumed_budget_minutes: float
    fast_burn_rate: float   # Current 1-hour burn rate
    slow_burn_rate: float   # Current 72-hour burn rate

    def remaining_pct(self) -> float:
        if self.total_budget_minutes == 0:
            return 100.0
        remaining = (
            self.total_budget_minutes - self.consumed_budget_minutes
        )
        return max(0.0, remaining / self.total_budget_minutes * 100)

    def days_until_exhaustion(self) -> Optional[float]:
        """Estimate days until budget exhaustion at slow burn rate."""
        remaining_pct = self.remaining_pct()
        if remaining_pct <= 0:
            return 0.0
        if self.slow_burn_rate <= 1.0:
            return None  # Not approaching exhaustion

        # At slow burn rate: how many days until 0%?
        # Budget decreases at: (slow_burn_rate - 1) * normal_rate
        # normal_rate = total_budget_minutes / (window_days * 24 * 60)
        normal_hourly_rate = (
            self.total_budget_minutes / (self.slo_config.window_days * 24)
        )
        excess_hourly_consumption = (
            (self.slow_burn_rate - 1) * normal_hourly_rate
        )
        if excess_hourly_consumption <= 0:
            return None
        remaining_minutes = (
            self.total_budget_minutes * remaining_pct / 100
        )
        hours_remaining = remaining_minutes / excess_hourly_consumption
        return hours_remaining / 24

    def policy_state(self) -> str:
        pct = self.remaining_pct()
        if pct > 50:
            return "NORMAL"
        elif pct > 20:
            return "MONITOR"
        elif pct > 10:
            return "WARNING"
        elif pct > 0:
            return "FEATURE_FREEZE"
        else:
            return "EMERGENCY"

⚠️ Common Misconceptions

MisconceptionReality
“Error budget policy means feature work always loses to reliability”Error budget policy means feature work is constrained when reliability is at risk. When error budget is > 50% (NORMAL state), there are no constraints on feature work. The policy only restricts features when the service is actively consuming reliability capacity. If reliability is healthy (budget remaining), features can proceed freely. The policy is not adversarial to product velocity - it prevents the specific scenario where features would cause SLO violations
“The feature freeze is too extreme - we can negotiate case-by-case”Case-by-case negotiation is the alternative to having a policy. Without a policy: every deploy gets negotiated individually, reliability loses the negotiation most of the time (feature urgency wins), and the SLO becomes a measurement rather than a constraint. The policy is what makes the SLO meaningful. The override mechanism (joint VP Engineering + SRE Lead approval) IS the case-by-case process - but it requires explicit accountability and agreement, not informal negotiation
“Error budget reset at the end of the month means policy violations have no consequences”Budget reset means the error budget is renewed (not punishment-based). The consequences are behavioral: incidents during a policy-enforced reliability sprint are thoroughly analyzed; action items from the reliability sprint address root causes; if the same service exceeds its error budget in consecutive months, the policy escalates (quarterly review, architecture change, additional SRE resources). The reset mechanism prevents the policy from becoming punitive while preserving the accountability structure
“The burn rate formula is complicated and our team won’t understand it”The burn rate concept is simple: “how fast are we consuming our error budget relative to the SLO rate?” A burn rate of 1 means we’re consuming budget exactly at the SLO rate (will exhaust at the end of the window). A burn rate of 14 means we’ll exhaust 10% of the monthly budget in 1 hour. For sprint planning: translate burn rate into the budget remaining percentage and days to exhaustion. “We have 8% budget left and will exhaust it in 2 days at the current rate” is universally understandable
“Phase 2 requires sophisticated tooling - we need to build a custom dashboard”Phase 2 can be implemented with basic tools. A minimum viable error budget dashboard: a Grafana panel showing remaining budget percentage and burn rate. A minimum viable policy: a Google Doc with the threshold table and a Slack channel notification when budget crosses 20%. Sprint planning integration: a standing agenda item. The minimum viable implementation takes a day, not a sprint. Sophistication (recording rules, automated policy enforcement, Jira integration) can be added iteratively
